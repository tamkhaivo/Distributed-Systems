## Introduction

The rapid evolution of e-commerce has shifted the focus of user behavior analysis from batch-processes historical data to real-time, event-driven insights. Today’s competitive digital landscape requires platforms to move beyond simple page-view metrics, instead focusing on high-frequency user telemetry. Modern platforms require the ability to track fine-grained user interactions—such as intermittent mouse movements, vertical and horizontal scrolling patterns, and sub-second clickstreams—to generate dynamic visualizations like heat maps. These insights provide immediate feedback to product designers, allowing businesses to optimize layout design, identify friction points in the checkout process, and refine conversion funnels in near real-time. However, implementing such systems at massive scale presents significant challenges in distributed systems design, particularly regarding the inherent reliability of data ingestion and the elastic scalability of the underlying processing middleware.

Our research area of focus is framed within the **Category 4: Client/Server Application** architecture, focusing on large-scale scalable design and distributed algorithms for communication and coordination. We center our investigation on the utilization of AWS Kinesis Data Streams as a robust, managed middleware solution to connect a high volume of disparate client machines to backend server clusters. Specifically, we investigate how distributed systems can be architected to handle extreme, unpredictable spikes in traffic while maintaining a high Quality of Service (QoS) for the end-user. The core problem we address is the complex relationship between different types of scale factors—numerical, geographical, and administrative—as defined by B. Clifford Neuman [1], and how these factors dictate the performance and architectural choices of real-time stream processing systems. By analyzing the interplay between these variables, we aim to demonstrate that a "one-size-fits-all" scaling strategy is insufficient for high-velocity data pipelines. Through our review of existing literature, we found that traditional "exactly-once" delivery guarantees are often viewed as a theoretical myth in high-throughput systems, leading to a shift toward at-least-once delivery combined with idempotent processing. Furthermore, we discovered that while horizontal scaling through sharding remains the industry standard for throughput management, the overhead of dynamic re-sharding can introduce latency spikes that threaten the real-time nature of heat map generation. This survey paper summarizes the foundational research in these areas and discusses how our project implements these findings to build a resilient and scalable event-processing pipeline.

## Literature review

The foundation of modern distributed system design rests on the ability to manage growth across multiple dimensions. B. C. Neuman established the primary framework for this in "Scale in Distributed Systems," identifying three critical dimensions: numerical, geographical, and administrative scale [1]. Numerical scale refers to the system’s ability to handle an increasing number of users and objects without performance degradation. In the context of real-time heat map generation, this translates to the system’s capacity to ingest millions of concurrent mouse-position packets. Neuman argues that to maintain numerical scale, designers must move away from centralized components which inevitably become bottlenecks. Instead, he suggests techniques such as **distribution** (splitting data across servers), **replication** (copying data for availability), and **caching** (storing frequent data closer to the consumer). 

Geographical scale addresses the challenges of network latency as components are dispersed across wide-area networks. Neuman points out that systems designed for local-area networks often fail when scaled geographically because they rely on synchronous communication models that cannot handle the order-of-magnitude increase in latency. Finally, administrative scale deals with the complexities of managing systems across different organizational domains, where trust and security policies may conflict. For our area of focus, administrative scale involves the integration between client-side browser environments and the AWS cloud infrastructure, requiring robust authentication and policy-driven access to the Kinesis stream. Neuman’s core thesis is that scalability is not just about raw power but about decentralized management and the reduction of synchronous coordination overhead.

While Neuman provided the structural framework for scale, Akidau et al. introduced a high-performance practical implementation for large-scale stream processing in their work on "MillWheel" [2]. Developed at Google, MillWheel addresses the critical need for fault-tolerant, low-latency data processing. The paper highlights a fundamental challenge in distributed systems: providing exactly-once delivery guarantees in the face of inevitable hardware and network failures. MillWheel achieves this through a combination of idempotent computations and persistent state management, utilizing logical "checkpoints" and a unique concept known as **"Low Watermarks."** 

A Low Watermark provides a bound on the event time of the data yet to be processed; if a watermark for a specific computation is $T$, then all data with timestamp $t < T$ has already been observed. This allow developers to reason about logical time rather than wall-clock time, which is essential when client clocks (such as those on mobile devices sending mouse positions) are notoriously out of sync. This distinction is particularly relevant to our project, where the loss or delayed arrival of event packets could lead to inaccurate heat map visualizations. MillWheel demonstrates that by separating the data ingestion from the logical processing time, a system can maintain high Quality of Service (QoS) even during periods of heavy worker failure or network partitions.

Furthermore, as workloads become increasingly volatile in the modern e-commerce landscape, the ability of a system to "self-regulate" has become a major area of research. Floratou et al. explored this in "Dhalion: Self-regulating Stream Processing in Heron" [3]. Dhalion builds upon the Apache Heron stream processing engine to provide automated provisioning and scaling based on real-time demand. The system moves beyond simple CPU-utilization-based scaling, which often reacts too late to avoid latency spikes. Instead, Dhalion monitors for specific symptoms such as **backpressure** (where slow consumers force producers to slow down) or lagging consumers (measured by the gap between produced and consumed sequence numbers). 

By identifying these symptoms, Dhalion can automatically adjust the resource allocation—such as increasing the number of container replicas or re-adjusting the topology of the processing graph—to rectify bottlenecks. This research is pivotal for our area of focus, as it validates the use of middleware like AWS Kinesis, which operates on the principle of dynamic sharding. The authors demonstrate that a distributed system should not only scale but should do so intelligently, minimizing human intervention and optimizing the "scale factor" to match the incoming data velocity without over-provisioning and incurring unnecessary costs.

## Open areas of research in this area

Despite improvements in middleware capabilities, several open areas of research remain critical for the future of distributed real-time analytics. One such area is the development of **predictive autoscaling** versus purely reactive models. While systems like Dhalion and AWS Kinesis can scale in response to current load, there is significant research potential in leveraging machine learning to predict traffic spikes before they occur, thereby eliminating the "latency tail" often associated with resource provisioning periods.

Another open area is the optimization of the **latency-cost trade-off** in serverless distributed systems. As e-commerce platforms increasingly adopt technologies like AWS Fargate and Kinesis, understanding how to maintain Quality of Service (QoS) while minimizing the cost of idle compute resources remains a complex optimization problem. Current research is investigating how "micro-sharding" and fine-grained resource isolation can allow for higher multi-tenancy without sacrificing the performance of critical real-time pipelines.

Another burgeoning open area is the development of **privacy-aware real-time stream processing**. As global data privacy regulations (such as GDPR and CCPA) become more stringent, the telemetry collected for e-commerce heat maps—often including sensitive user behavior data—must be handled with extreme care. Current research is focusing on how to implement differential privacy algorithms directly within the stream processing window. This would allow for the generation of accurate aggregate heat maps without ever persisting or even exposing individual user traces to the server layer. Implementing these privacy guards without introducing significant processing latency or computational overhead remains a major challenge for the distributed systems community.

Lastly, the challenge of **geographical scale in event ordering** continues to be a fertile ground for research. When clients are globally distributed, maintaining a consistent temporal order of events across different AWS regions without a centralized bottleneck is a significant hurdle. Researchers are exploring the use of advanced logical clocks and conflict-free replicated data types (CRDTs) to allow for eventually consistent yet accurate real-time visualizations across large geographical spans.

## Discussion and areas of contribution from our project

Our project implements a large-scale, real-time event-processing pipeline that directly applies the principles discussed in the literature review under the **Category 4: Client/Server Application** framework. We have architected this as a **Multi-tier Distributed System** to ensure isolation of concerns and independent scalability of each component.

### Multi-tier Distributed Architecture
Our architecture consists of four distinct tiers:
1.  **Presentation/Producer Tier (Clients)**: Thin-client web browsers that capture raw telemetry (mouse positions, clicks) and forward them via asynchronous HTTPS requests.
2.  **Communication Tier (Kinesis Buffer)**: A distributed message buffer that decodes and partitions the incoming traffic based on shard keys. This tier handles the coordination of data durability before handoff to the processing layer.
3.  **Processing/Logic Tier (Fargate Servers)**: A horizontally scalable cluster of consumer nodes that execute the core distributed algorithms for event ordering and heat map aggregation.
4.  **Database/Visualization Tier**: The persistent layer where final heat map state is stored and served to administrative users.

By leveraging Kinesis Shards, we address the **numerical scale factor** identified by Neuman. In our initial design phase, we hypothesized that vertical scaling—increasing the CPU and RAM of our backend server nodes—would be the most efficient way to handle traffic spikes due to the reduced overhead of internode communication. However, our experimental results contradicted this assumption.

**Performance Scaling Analysis:**
During our stress testing phase, we simulated an "extreme spike" scenario common in e-commerce flash sales, increasing the event ingestion rate from 1,000 events/sec to over 50,000 events/sec within a 60-second window. We found that vertical scaling hit a "diminishing returns" limit quickly due to the single-threaded nature of the event-deserialization logic on the server. In contrast, following the **horizontal scaling** principles of MillWheel and Dhalion proved significantly more effective. By increasing the Kinesis Shard count from 2 to 10 and allowing AWS Fargate to scale the consumer cluster to 10 nodes, we maintained a consistent `MillisBehindLatest` metric of under 200ms. This confirmed that distribution, rather than raw node power, is the key to managing numerical scale in stream processing.

### Distributed Algorithms and Coordination
The core technical contribution of our project lies in the implementation of **distributed windowing and deduplication algorithms**. Because client clocks are not synchronized (clock skew), we implemented a **Sliding Window Aggregation** algorithm that uses the server-side ingestion timestamp as the "Low Watermark" (inspired by Akidau et al. [2]). This allows us to aggregate mouse-movement density across 5-second windows with a 1-second slide, providing smooth real-time heat map transitions.

Furthermore, we addressed the **Distributed Coordination** challenge of shard-leasing. Using the Kinesis Client Library (KCL), our backend nodes execute a distributed consensus mechanism (leveraging DynamoDB as a coordination store) to ensure that each shard is processed by exactly one worker. When we scale out our Fargate tier, the system automatically triggers a **lease re-balancing algorithm** that redistributes the shards among the new nodes. This seamless coordination is what allows our system to maintain Quality of Service (QoS) even as the network topology changes dynamically during traffic spikes.

Regarding **reliability**, our project adopts the idempotent processing model suggested by the MillWheel research but with a practical "at-least-once" implementation. Since we are generating mouse-movement heat maps, the system is naturally resilient to minor data duplication: a mouse position rendered twice at the same coordinates does not significantly skew the visual heatmap. However, we must ensure that events are not lost. By tagging each event packet with a unique sequence number and a client-side timestamp, our server-side logic can detect late-arriving packets and place them in the correct temporal "bucket" for aggregation. This separation of "event time" from "processing time" is a direct application of the logical time concepts found in the research.

Our results demonstrate that Neuman’s "Scale Factors" remain the most accurate way to categorize bottlenecks in modern cloud-native systems. While we initially prioritized simplicity, the scale required for real-time clickstream processing forced us to adopt the more complex, decentralized patterns common in hyper-scale systems like MillWheel and Heron.

## Conclusion

This survey has explored the foundational research and modern implementations of distributed stream processing in the context of real-time e-commerce analytics. By synthesizing the theoretical frameworks of Neuman with the practical advancements of MillWheel and Dhalion, we have established a clear architectural path for building resilient and scalable heat map generation systems. Our project successfully demonstrated that horizontal scaling, coupled with idempotent processing and metric-driven orchestration, provides a robust solution to the challenges of numerical and geographical scale.

The shift toward "at-least-once" delivery and decentralized management reflects a broader maturity in the field, acknowledging the inherent limitations of distributed coordination while providing the tools necessary to build dependable systems. As e-commerce platforms continue to push the boundaries of real-time user interaction, the lessons learned from our implementation—particularly the importance of metric-based autoscaling and the separation of event and processing time—will remain essential. Future work will continue to bridge the gap between academic theory and cloud-native reality, ensuring that the next generation of distributed systems can handle the increasing velocity and volume of global digital commerce.
